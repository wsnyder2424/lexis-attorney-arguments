I. Introduction

Defendant Thompson Inc. respectfully submits this Response Brief in opposition to Plaintiffs' Expedited Motion for an Order Granting J. Carl Cooper access to Defendant's confidential infringement documents. Plaintiffs have failed to demonstrate the requisite good cause to justify such extraordinary expedited discovery, and the relief sought would impose undue burden on Defendant while causing serious prejudice to ongoing licensing negotiations.

For these reasons, the oil companies' reliance upon Cannon v. New Orleans (1874) 87 U.S. (20 Wall.) 577, and Harman v. Chicago (1983) 147 U.S. 396, and upon certain dicta in Packet Co. v. Keokuk (1877) 95 U.S. 80, is misplaced. Link to the text of the note neither Cannon nor Harmon nor the cited dicta in Keokuk pertain to charges to the provision of specific property or services.

II. Background

This Court has held that where there is an exclusive private appropriation of public property, a special charge to the benefitted [*6] party is entirely proper, regardless of the nature of the commerce in which the party is engaged. (St. Louis v. Western Union Telegraph Co. (1893) 148 U.S. 92, 98–99). Link to the text of the note

Plaintiffs filed the underlying complaint on December 4, 2004, alleging willful patent infringement arising from Defendant's manufacture and sale of industrial fluid management systems. Shortly thereafter, Plaintiffs filed the present motion seeking immediate access to a broad category of documents that they characterize as "infringement documents," without defining the scope of that category or establishing relevance to any particular claim.

III. Argument

The standard for expedited discovery requires Plaintiffs to show: (1) a likelihood of success on the merits; (2) irreparable injury absent expedited discovery; (3) the balance of hardships tips in their favor; and (4) the public interest favors the relief. Semitool, Inc. v. Tokyo Electron Am., Inc., 208 F.R.D. 273, 275–76 (N.D. Cal. 2002).

Plaintiffs have satisfied none of these requirements. The motion is devoid of any analysis of the merits of their infringement claims. Plaintiffs do not even attempt to demonstrate a likelihood of success; instead they rely on conclusory assertions that access to the documents will advance their case. This is insufficient as a matter of law. Link to the text of the note

Furthermore, Plaintiffs have not demonstrated that they would suffer irreparable harm if forced to proceed through normal discovery channels. Courts in this district have consistently held that a party's mere desire for early access to documents does not constitute irreparable harm sufficient to justify expedited relief.

IV. Conclusion

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant Thompson Inc. respectfully requests that this Court deny Plaintiffs' Expedited Motion in its entirety and award Defendant its costs and fees incurred in opposing this motion pursuant to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 37.
